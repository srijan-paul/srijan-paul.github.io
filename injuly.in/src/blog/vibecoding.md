I was on a phone call with a friend when one of us brought up the subject of AI coding agents.
During our conversation, I articulated something I'd never forced myself to think about – "Why do I love using LLMs for work but feel negatively about the term 'Vibecoding'?"
Once you spend years honing a craft, you start to identify with it.
People associating your identity with 'vibes' and equating your capability to that of a chatbot feels offensive.
And this is perhaps why the term sometimes elicits an almost visceral reaction from people who identify as an SWE.

Vibecoding is also easy to misconstrue as a lack of rigor. Yes, it requires great discipline to remain rigorous when code flies by faster than you can read, but I still believe that it's possible to uphold the same old quality standards when using AI. \[1: pr conventions\]

Since coding agents have become a subject of discourse outside the tech sphere, I'll add my voice to the choir:

## Software engineering is changing fundamentally.

A year ago I was an LLM-coding skeptic, unable to get any value out of apps like Cursor. When I gave these tools a fair chance, it would end with me leaving the monitor frustrated. The auto-complete suggestions would feel intrusive and annoying. For a while I went back to coding without AI.

Sometime in the middle of 2025, I wound up using Ampcode at work to learn about recent developments in ML. I'd git clone repos of some research papers, then chat with Amp about the implementation. Once I noticed how much better it had gotten at *understanding* code, I started using it for programming again. Just the tedious bits at first, but then some bigger chunks too. As of late, I've noticed something remarkable – the 'weight' of a workpiece I can offload to coding agents has been steadily growing and now stands at a size I can't ignore.  At the time of writing, I'm *at least* 2-3x as fast when working with coding agents.

## The new unit economics of software

Say you're a company that deals with sensitive government data, and you need an offline, no-cloud ticketing system with ID verification on log-in. Usually even a simple CRUD project like this would require engaging with a consulting firm for several months, just because the use case is so specific. I posit that by 2027, most greenfield projects will be delivered by a single in-house developer using AI.

This isn't just limited to software consulting, of course. AI also enables a great deal of purpose-built software.

A few months ago I needed to clean, preview, and de-duplicate a relatively large dataset at work. I tried a few existing tools on GitHub that didn't meet my needs, then prompted Amp into building exactly what I needed in a single afternoon. This application now saves me about an hour of running bash scripts to clean and merge datasets every week, while I get to focus on improving my ML model's architecture/training loop.

## How to feel about all this as a hobbyist?

Some people are outcome-oriented, while others are process-oriented. Those obsessed with the outcome couldn't get AGI sooner, while those who enjoyed the *process* of creating software might develop a sense of unease around all that is happening.
